The Subject

About the boy in the red hunting hat.

My name is Holden. I'm sixteen and I'm pretty tall for my age — about six foot two and a half — with this one side of my hair that's gray, which is funny if you stop and think about it. I was kicked out of Pencey Prep, which is the fourth school I've flunked out of if you really want to know, and I don't much feel like going home to tell my parents about it.

I'm not going to tell you my whole goddam autobiography or anything. I'll just tell you about this madman stuff that happened to me around last Christmas, before I got pretty run-down and had to come out here and take it easy. The thing is, I bought this red hunting hat in New York that morning we had the fencing meet, and I wear it sometimes when I'm feeling a certain way. People stare, but I don't give a damn.

I'm taking pictures now. Mostly because words run out on you eventually, especially when you're trying to say something about kids, or about phonies, or about how everything keeps changing when you'd rather it just stayed put. So I take pictures. Of museums. Of ducks. Of empty rooms. Of my sister Phoebe, when she'll let me. Each one is a little piece of the field, if you know what I mean. I'm trying to catch them before they fall.

— Holden C.

The Plates.

Nine photographs after the novel — each one an attempt to look at the things Holden could not stop looking at: the small, the lonely, the disappearing.

Plate I
i Manhattan · December

The red hunting hat on a hotel desk.

A small and ridiculous object — bought for a dollar in a store on Broadway — becomes the most reliable thing he owns. When he wears it backwards, he chooses how he is seen. When he takes it off in front of others, he is hiding. The hat is Holden's private armor against a world he cannot trust to look at him kindly.

This is a people shooting hat. I shoot people in this hat. — Chapter 3
Plate II
ii Central Park · The Lagoon

Where do the ducks go?

Holden asks the question to two cab drivers. Neither has an answer, and neither wants one. But the question is never really about ducks. It is about whether the small, vulnerable creatures of this world get taken care of when the cold sets in — and, by extension, whether anybody is going to take care of him.

I was wondering where the ducks went when the lagoon got all icy and frozen over. — Chapter 2
Plate III
iii Central Park · Carousel · Rain

Phoebe on the carousel.

She rides the brown horse around and around, reaching for the gold ring. He sits on the bench and gets soaked through, and for the first time in the book he is almost happy. The lesson is one he resists for three hundred pages: you cannot keep children from grabbing for the gold ring. To love them is to let them risk falling.

The thing with kids is, if they want to grab for the gold ring, you have to let them do it. — Chapter 25
Plate IV
iv Museum of Natural History

The diorama, unchanged.

The Eskimo is still fishing. The deer are still drinking. The squaw with the bare bosom is still weaving. Holden cannot bear that he himself, walking past the same case at twelve and again at sixteen, will not be the same. The museum holds out an impossible promise — that something, somewhere, can be kept exactly as it was.

The best thing, though, in that museum was that everything always stayed right where it was. — Chapter 16
Plate V
v A field, in his head

The catcher in the rye.

The whole title is rooted here. He pictures a field on the edge of a cliff, full of children running, none of them looking. He is standing at the edge to grab whoever comes too close. It is the only thing he says he would really like to be — and the only job in the book that does not yet exist.

I keep picturing all these little kids playing some game in this big field of rye. — Chapter 22
Plate VI
vi Edmont Hotel · 3 a.m.

A window, three in the morning.

He has the room. He has the cigarettes. He has the cash, the highball, the phone book full of numbers. What he does not have is anyone he can stand to call. The city outside the window is full of laughter and lit windows, and somehow that is the worst part. Loneliness in a crowd is a different and lonelier kind.

New York's terrible when somebody laughs on the street very late at night. — Chapter 12
Plate VII
vii Phoebe's school · stairwell wall

An inscription he tried to rub out.

He sees it scratched into a wall outside his sister's school and rubs at it with his hand until his hand hurts. He sees it again, scratched in deeper this time, on a different wall. He realizes the truth that finally undoes him: every field has a corner where someone has already written something cruel, and you cannot rub them all out, even if you had a million years.

You couldn't rub out even half the marks. It's hopeless, anyway. — Chapter 25
Plate VIII
viii Pencey Prep · empty classroom

A room full of phonies.

Holden's word for what is wrong with everyone is "phony" — meaning insincere, performing, faking it for the sake of being seen. The school, the dorm, the headmaster shaking hands differently with rich and poor parents — all of it grinds him down. The empty room is what he sees when he looks past the people in it.

One of the biggest reasons I left Elkton Hills was because I was surrounded by phonies. — Chapter 2
Plate IX
ix Phoebe's room · afternoon

The girl who listens.

Phoebe is the only person Holden does not lie to. She is ten and reads everything and writes books about a girl detective named Hazle. When she demands he tell her one thing — just one thing — that he actually likes, the answer he eventually finds is her. The novel's tenderness lives in the room of a child who refuses to let her brother off easy.

She kills me. She really does. — Chapter 10
Coda

Don't ever tell anybody anything. If you do, you start missing everybody.

— J. D. Salinger, final lines
